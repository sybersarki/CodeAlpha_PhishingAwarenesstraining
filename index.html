Module 01

What is Phishing?

Phishing is a cyberattack where criminals impersonate trusted entities to steal sensitive information like passwords, credit card numbers, or personal data.

🎣

The Concept

Like fishing with bait, attackers "cast" fake messages hoping victims will "bite" and reveal sensitive information or click malicious links.

🎭

Impersonation

Attackers disguise themselves as banks, government agencies, tech companies, or even your colleagues to gain your trust.

⚡

Urgency Tactics

Messages create panic — "Your account will be suspended!", "Verify now or lose access!" — pressuring victims to act without thinking.

🔗

Malicious Payloads

Links lead to fake websites that harvest credentials, or attachments install malware that gives attackers full access to your system.

Module 02

Types of Phishing Attacks

Attackers use many different vectors. Knowing each type is the first step to defending against them.

High Risk

Email Phishing

Mass emails impersonating legitimate organizations sent to thousands of targets hoping some will click.

High Risk

Spear Phishing

Highly targeted attacks using personal information about the victim to make messages more convincing.

High Risk

Whaling

Spear phishing targeting executives and high-value individuals — "big fish" in an organisation.

Medium Risk

Smishing

SMS-based phishing using text messages with malicious links pretending to be delivery services or banks.

Medium Risk

Vishing

Voice phishing — phone calls from fake bank agents, tech support, or government officials requesting sensitive data.

Emerging

Clone Phishing

A legitimate email is cloned with malicious links replacing the originals and resent from a spoofed address.

Module 03

Social Engineering Tactics

Phishing succeeds not by hacking computers — but by hacking human psychology. These are the manipulation tactics attackers use.

⏰

Urgency & Scarcity

"Your account will be locked in 24 hours!" Attackers manufacture time pressure to force victims into acting before thinking critically. The panic overrides rational decision-making.

🤝

Authority & Impersonation

Messages appear to come from CEOs, banks, HMRC, or IT departments. People naturally comply with perceived authority figures — attackers exploit this obedience instinct.

😨

Fear & Intimidation

"You owe unpaid taxes — pay now or face arrest." Fear triggers the fight-or-flight response, bypassing logical thinking and pushing victims toward immediate compliance.

🎁

Greed & Reward

"You've won £500! Click to claim." The promise of reward clouds judgment. If something sounds too good to be true, it almost certainly is a social engineering trap.

💬

Familiarity & Trust

Attackers research victims on LinkedIn, social media, and company websites to craft messages that reference real colleagues, projects, or events — making the attack feel personal and credible.

🙏

Reciprocity

"I've done something for you — now I need a small favour." Attackers build rapport first, then exploit the human tendency to want to return favours, especially in workplace settings.

Real-World Examples

Attacks That Actually Happened

These are real documented phishing incidents showing how devastating social engineering can be.

$100M Lost

Facebook & Google (2013–2015)

A Lithuanian hacker impersonated a Taiwanese hardware vendor via fake invoices. Both tech giants wired over $100 million before discovering the fraud. Pure social engineering — no malware used.

CEO Fraud

FACC Austrian Aerospace (2016)

Attackers impersonated the CEO via email and instructed a finance employee to transfer €50 million for a "secret acquisition project." The employee complied. The CEO and CFO were later fired.

Spear Phishing

Sony Pictures Hack (2014)

Attackers sent targeted spear phishing emails to Sony employees. Once credentials were stolen, hackers gained full network access — leaking films, salaries, and private executive emails worldwide.

NHS Attack

WannaCry Ransomware (2017)

Delivered initially via phishing emails, WannaCry crippled the UK's NHS — cancelling 19,000 appointments and costing £92 million. Attackers exploited unpatched systems after initial phishing entry.

Twitter Hack

Twitter VIP Account Hack (2020)

Attackers used phone-based social engineering (vishing) to trick Twitter employees into providing internal system access. Accounts of Obama, Biden, Musk, and Apple were hijacked in minutes.

Smishing

Royal Mail Parcel Scam (Ongoing)

Millions of UK residents receive fake Royal Mail SMS messages requesting £1.99 to "release a parcel." The small amount lowers suspicion — but the real goal is harvesting card details.

Module 04

Spotting Fake Websites

Phishing emails lead to fake websites designed to steal your credentials. Here's how to identify them before it's too late.

🔍

Check the URL Carefully

Fake sites use subtle tricks: paypa1.com (number 1), arnazon.com (rn looks like m), secure-barclays.xyz (wrong TLD). Always read the full domain character by character.

🔒

HTTPS Is Not Enough

A padlock does NOT mean a website is safe — it only means your connection is encrypted. Attackers can and do use HTTPS on phishing sites. Over 80% of phishing sites now use HTTPS.

🎨

Cloned Visual Design

Modern phishing sites are pixel-perfect copies of real ones. Attackers simply copy the HTML/CSS of legitimate sites. Visual appearance alone cannot be trusted as a safety indicator.

📋

Unusual Form Requests

Legitimate sites never ask for your full password, PIN, or all card details on one page. If a site asks for information that feels excessive — close it immediately and navigate to the real site manually.

Module 05

Recognizing a Phishing Email

Hover over the highlighted sections below to learn why they are red flags.

Inbox — Mail Client
From:
security-alert@barc1ays-verify.com
⚠️ Fake domain! Real Barclays emails come from @barclays.com. The "1" instead of "l" and extra "-verify" are classic spoofing tactics.
To: customer@youremail.com
Subject:
URGENT: Your account has been compromised — Verify immediately
⚠️ Urgency language is a manipulation tactic. Legitimate banks don't use "URGENT" or pressure you to act immediately.

Dear Valued Customer,


We have detected suspicious login activity on your account. Your account has been temporarily limited.


To restore full access, click here to verify your identity within 24 hours or your account will be permanently suspended.
⚠️ Artificial deadline creates panic. "24 hours or else" is designed to stop you thinking clearly.

👉 [Verify My Account Now] — http://barclays-secure-login.xyz/verify
⚠️ The URL doesn't match the real domain. Always check the actual URL — ".xyz" domains are commonly used in phishing. Hover before you click!

If you do not verify, we will be forced to permanently close you're account.
⚠️ Grammar error! "you're" should be "your". Phishing emails often contain spelling/grammar mistakes — banks proofread their communications.

Barclays Security Team

📧

Suspicious Sender Address

Check the full email domain — not just the display name. Look for misspellings or extra words.

🚨

Urgency & Threats

"Act now or lose access" — this pressure tactic is designed to override your critical thinking.

🔗

Suspicious Links

Always hover over links before clicking. The displayed text and actual URL should match.

✍️

Grammar & Spelling Errors

Legitimate organisations proofread carefully. Errors are a telltale sign of phishing attempts.

📎

Unexpected Attachments

Never open attachments you weren't expecting — especially .exe, .zip, or macro-enabled Office files.

🎁

Too Good To Be True

"You've won £1,000!" — if you didn't enter a competition, you didn't win. Delete it immediately.

Module 06

How to Protect Yourself

Follow these best practices to dramatically reduce your risk of becoming a phishing victim.

01

Enable Multi-Factor Authentication

Even if attackers steal your password, MFA stops them accessing your account without your second factor.

02

Verify Before You Click

When in doubt, go directly to the website by typing the URL manually rather than clicking any link.

03

Use a Password Manager

Password managers won't autofill on fake sites — they only fill on the real domain, protecting you automatically.

04

Keep Software Updated

Updates patch vulnerabilities that phishing malware exploits. Enable automatic updates on all devices.

05

Report Suspicious Emails

Report phishing to your IT team and use the "Report Phishing" button in your email client to protect others.

06

Use Anti-Phishing Tools

Browser extensions and email filters can automatically detect and block known phishing sites and emails.

07

Think Before You Act

Pause before responding to any urgent request. Legitimate organizations will never pressure you to act in seconds.

08

Verify Requests Independently

If your "bank" emails you, call them directly using the number on their official website — not one in the email.
